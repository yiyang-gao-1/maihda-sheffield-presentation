What is MAIHDA?

3/10

Multilevel Analysis of Individual Heterogeneity and Discriminatory Accuracy

The "Gold Standard" for Quantitative Intersectional Analysis

Treats intersectional identities as random effects in multilevel models, revealing how multiple characteristics combine to create unique experiences beyond simple additive effects

Conventional Approach:

  • Examines factors separately
  • Assumes additive effects
  • Misses critical intersections
  • Generic policy recommendations

MAIHDA Approach:

  • Creates intersectional strata
  • Reveals multiplicative effects
  • Handles small cell sizes via shrinkage
  • Enables targeted interventions

Three Extended MAIHDA Applications:

  • Spatial MAIHDA → School Segregation Study
  • Longitudinal MAIHDA → Teacher Retention Study
  • Policy Evaluation MAIHDA → Transport & Accessibility Study

Study 1: Educational Segregation - Current Approach

4/10

Research Question

Which students experience educational segregation, and how does this vary across space and time?

Current Aggregate Approach:

  • • Calculates: Segregation indices by ethnicity and area
  • • Shows: Overall levels of ethnic concentration in schools
  • • Misses: Which specific students attend segregated schools
  • • Cannot reveal: Intersectional vulnerabilities
boot_model <- glmer(
  attends_segregated ~ 1 +
  (1 | strata_id) + # intersectional strata
  (1 | lsoa_id), # spatial clusters
  family = binomial,
  data = boot_students,
  control = glmerControl(optimizer = "bobyqa",
    optCtrl = list(maxfun = 10000))
)

The Segregation Paradox

Aggregate data shows decreasing segregation, but which students still experience it?

Who Attends Segregated Schools?

Current methods can't answer:

  • Are Pakistani boys more affected than Pakistani girls?
  • Does social class intersect with ethnicity?
  • Where do vulnerable groups cluster?

Definition of Segregated School:

Schools where >70% of students are from a single ethnic group (following Burgess & Wilson, 2005)

Study 1: Spatial MAIHDA Enhancement

5/10

Individual-Level Spatial MAIHDA Innovation

Method Enhancement:

  • Unit: Individual students (N = 100k)
  • Outcome: Attends segregated school (binary)
  • 24 intersectional strata: Ethnicity × SES × Gender
  • Cross-classified with spatial units (LSOAs)
  • Monte Carlo: 10,000 simulations

Discriminatory Accuracy: ICC = 0.15

85% of variation is within intersectional groups - context matters more than identity alone

Critical Finding: Monte Carlo reveals Pakistani low-SES boys have 71.2% [95% CI: 68.4%-73.8%] probability of attending segregated schools, with strong spatial clustering (Moran's I = 0.82) in specific urban areas. This intersectional vulnerability was invisible in aggregate analyses.

Study 2: Teacher Retention - Current Approach

6/10

Tracking the 2011 Teacher Cohort: Traditional Survival Analysis

Current Method Limitations:

  • Cox model with additive effects
  • Assumes: ethnicity + gender + ITT = risk
  • Misses intersectional experiences
  • Cannot identify time-specific vulnerabilities

Longitudinal MAIHDA Model:

# Fit Longitudinal MAIHDA with time-varying effects
long_maihda <- glmer(
  event_this_year ~ year_scaled + year2 +
  (year_scaled + year2 | strata_id) + # Random slopes
  (1 | school_id) + # School effects
  (1 | teacher_id), # Individual frailty
  family = binomial(link = "cloglog"),
  data = teachers_pp,
  control = glmerControl(optimizer = "bobyqa",
    optCtrl = list(maxfun = 50000))
)

Study 2: Longitudinal MAIHDA Enhancement (Bell et al., 2024)

7/10

Revealing Intersectional Career Trajectories

Longitudinal MAIHDA Innovation:

  • 200 intersectional strata: Ethnicity × Gender × ITT × Region
  • Time-varying hazards: Captures non-linear career patterns
  • Individual trajectories: Random slopes for each stratum
  • Critical periods: Statistically identified vulnerability windows

Key Finding: 78% of variation is within intersectional groups - individual context dominates

Heterogeneous Career Trajectories:

■ Black × Male × Non-ITT × London (HR: 2.8 at Year 2)
■ Pakistani × Female × Non-ITT × North (HR: 2.3 at Year 1)
■ White × Female × ITT × North (Reference group)
■ Asian × Male × ITT × London (Most resilient)
Intersectional Group Year 1 Risk Year 2 Risk Critical Period 11-Year Retention
Black × Male × Non-ITT × London 18% 31% Year 2 Crisis 3.2%
Pakistani × Female × Non-ITT × North 22% 18% Year 1 Crisis 4.8%
White × Female × ITT × North 8% 10% Gradual 23.4%

Transformative Insight: Longitudinal MAIHDA reveals that "teacher retention" isn't one problem but many distinct challenges. Black male non-ITT teachers in London face a Year 2 crisis (HR = 2.8, 95% CrI: [2.1-3.7]), whilst Pakistani female non-ITT teachers in the North struggle most in Year 1. This enables precisely targeted, time-specific interventions.

Study 3: London Transport Policy - The MAIHDA Difference

8/10

London's Free School Transport Policy:

Free transport if: >3 miles from school OR >2 miles + low income OR 2-15 miles + faith school

❌ Without MAIHDA

Conclusion: "Policy is working well!"

What We Miss:

"Expand eligibility for all"

Reduction = (Baseline SD - New SD) / Baseline SD × 100%

🎯 Why Targeted Outreach Wins:

Because it addresses the actual barriers - information gaps, cultural mistrust, language issues. We're not changing eligibility; we're ensuring those who ARE eligible can actually access the service.

📊 The MAIHDA Insight:

MAIHDA reveals that what looks like a successful policy (65% average) actually masks severe inequalities. Without MAIHDA, we'd waste £10M making more people eligible who still wouldn't use the service. With MAIHDA, we invest £3M in targeted outreach and achieve 2.5× greater inequality reduction.

Main Takeaways

  1. Intersectional membership matters but explains modest variance (10-20%)
  2. Most variance (80-90%) exists within intersectional groups
  3. Beyond "colourblind" or "essentialist" (systematic inequalities + individual/contextual variation)
