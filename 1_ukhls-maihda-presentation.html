MAIHDA: Multilevel Analysis of Individual Heterogeneity and Discriminatory Accuracy

A powerful framework for understanding how social identities intersect to shape health outcomes

Overview: Three Extended MAIHDA Studies

Study 1: Longitudinal MAIHDA

Research Question: How do physical health trajectories differ across intersectional groups defined by sex, ethnicity, and education, and how do these trajectories vary with aging and time?

Key Innovation: Separates age effects (time-varying) from stratum membership (fixed)

Study 2: Spatial MAIHDA

Research Question: To what extent do geographic contexts amplify or mitigate mental health inequalities across intersectional groups?

Key Innovation: Cross-classified multilevel model examining place-based interventions

Study 3: Policy Evaluation MAIHDA

Research Question: How did COVID-19 differentially impact mental health across intersectional groups?

Key Innovation: Evaluates differential policy impacts using baseline controls

Study 1: Longitudinal MAIHDA - Physical Health Trajectories

Key Findings

  • Variance Decomposition: 7.03% of variance at stratum level in empty model, reduced to 0.11% in full model
  • Demographics explain 98.6% of stratum variance - suggesting largely additive effects
  • Age effect: -0.204 points per year on physical health (significant decline)
  • Individual trajectories vary significantly - random slopes improve model fit
  • Correlation of -0.387 between baseline health and age slopes - those with better initial health show steeper declines

    • Model Progression

    Empty Model → Basic Time → Age Trajectories → Fixed Effects → Random Slopes → Full Model

    Best model (by AIC/BIC): Full model with random slopes, demographics, and birth cohort effects

    Key Findings

    Methodological Innovations

    Longitudinal MAIHDA

    • Separates age effects from stratum membership
    • Models individual change and variation in trajectories
    • Reduces confounding through within-person comparisons
    • Can examine trajectories over time, not just static disparities

    Spatial MAIHDA

    • Cross-classified multilevel model
    • Accounts for geographic clustering
    • Tests cross-level interactions between area and individual characteristics
    • Identifies priority areas combining high prevalence and high inequality

    Policy Evaluation MAIHDA

    • Uses pre-pandemic baseline data with COVID-19 survey waves
    • Tests whether baseline characteristics moderate policy effects
    • Can identify which groups are most vulnerable to external shocks
    • Evaluates differential policy impacts across intersectional groups

    Overall Pattern Across Studies

    The Additive Main Effects Finding

    Across all three studies, the additive main effects of demographics explain most (75-99%) of the between-stratum variance

    What This Means

    • Intersectional effects are largely additive rather than interactive for these outcomes
    • Individual-level variation remains important - significant variation within strata
    • Contextual effects matter - time, place, and policy contexts shape outcomes
    • Traditional demographic categories capture most inequality but miss important nuances

    Policy Implications

    Universal vs Targeted Interventions

    From Spatial MAIHDA

    6 areas need "Universal + Targeted" interventions: High overall prevalence AND high inequality

    7 areas need "Monitor" only: Low prevalence and low inequality

    5 areas need "Universal" interventions: High prevalence but low inequality

    5 areas need "Targeted" interventions: Low prevalence but high inequality

    Key Recommendations

    • Physical Health: Focus on aging populations - steeper declines for those with initially good health
    • Mental Health: Geographic targeting essential - rural areas show better outcomes
    • COVID Support: Young females with lower education most vulnerable
    • Area-based interventions: Can address multiple intersecting disadvantages simultaneously

    Main Message: While intersectional effects are largely additive, understanding patterns across groups remains crucial for effective policy design and resource allocation

    Conclusions

    Key Contributions

  • Methodological Innovation: Three novel extensions of MAIHDA framework
  • Empirical Insights: Comprehensive understanding of health inequalities in UK
  • Policy Relevance: Clear guidance for intervention strategies

    • Main Takeaways

    1. Traditional demographic categories explain most between-group variance

    2. Individual trajectories and contextual factors remain crucial

    3. Different outcomes require different analytical approaches

    4. Extended MAIHDA methods offer valuable insights beyond conventional analysis

    Future Directions

    • Apply these methods to other health outcomes and contexts
    • Develop software tools for easier implementation
    • Explore higher-order interactions in larger samples
    • Integrate with causal inference frameworks
