What is MAIHDA?

3/10

Multilevel Analysis of Individual Heterogeneity and Discriminatory Accuracy

The "Gold Standard" for Quantitative Intersectional Analysis

Treats intersectional identities as random effects in multilevel models, revealing how multiple characteristics combine to create unique experiences beyond simple additive effects

Conventional Approach:

  • Examines factors separately
  • Assumes additive effects
  • Misses critical intersections
  • Generic policy recommendations

MAIHDA Approach:

  • Creates intersectional strata
  • Reveals multiplicative effects
  • Handles small cell sizes via shrinkage
  • Enables targeted interventions

Three Extended MAIHDA Applications:

  • Spatial MAIHDA ‚Üí School Segregation Study
  • Longitudinal MAIHDA ‚Üí Teacher Retention Study
  • Policy Evaluation MAIHDA ‚Üí Transport & Accessibility Study

Study 1: Educational Segregation - Current Approach

4/10

Research Question

Which students experience educational segregation, and how does this vary across space and time?

Current Aggregate Approach:

  • ‚Ä¢ Calculates: Segregation indices by ethnicity and area
  • ‚Ä¢ Shows: Overall levels of ethnic concentration in schools
  • ‚Ä¢ Misses: Which specific students attend segregated schools
  • ‚Ä¢ Cannot reveal: Intersectional vulnerabilities

Current Method

  • Aggregate segregation indices
  • Cannot identify individual risk factors
  • Misses within-group heterogeneity

The Segregation Paradox

Aggregate data shows decreasing segregation, but which students still experience it?

Who Attends Segregated Schools?

Current methods can't answer:

  • Are Pakistani boys more affected than Pakistani girls?
  • Does social class intersect with ethnicity?
  • Where do vulnerable groups cluster?

Definition of Segregated School:

Schools where >70% of students are from a single ethnic group (following Burgess & Wilson, 2005)

Limitation: Aggregate approaches hide intersectional experiences - we need individual-level analysis to understand WHO experiences segregation, not just HOW MUCH segregation exists

Study 2: Teacher Retention - Current Approach

6/10

Tracking the 2011 Teacher Cohort: Traditional Survival Analysis

Current Method Limitations:

  • Cox model with additive effects
  • Assumes: ethnicity + gender + ITT = risk
  • Misses intersectional experiences
  • Cannot identify time-specific vulnerabilities

Hidden Variation:

Overall retention rates mask huge differences:

  • Female: 16.54% - but ranges from 8% to 24% by intersectional group
  • No ITT: 9.75% - but some groups retain at 18%, others at 3%

Survival Curves for 2011 Cohort:

Critical Limitation: These aggregate curves hide intersectional vulnerabilities. A Black male teacher without ITT in London has a fundamentally different trajectory than a White female teacher with ITT in rural areas - but current analysis treats them as parts of separate, additive categories.

Study 2: Longitudinal MAIHDA Enhancement (Bell et al., 2024)

7/10

Revealing Intersectional Career Trajectories

Longitudinal MAIHDA Innovation:

  • 200 intersectional strata: Ethnicity √ó Gender √ó ITT √ó Region
  • Time-varying hazards: Captures non-linear career patterns
  • Individual trajectories: Random slopes for each stratum
  • Critical periods: Statistically identified vulnerability windows
logit(leaving) ~ poly(year,2) + (poly(year,2)|strata) + (1|school) + (1|teacher)

Key Finding: 78% of variation is within intersectional groups - individual context dominates

Study 3: London's Free School Transport Policy - Current Approach

8/10

Evaluating Complex Eligibility Criteria: Who Really Benefits?

London's Free Transport Policy:

Standard Eligibility:

  • Under 8: >2 miles to school
  • 8+: >3 miles to school
  • No safe walking route
  • Special educational needs

Low-Income Eligibility:

  • 8-11: >2 miles (nearest school)
  • 11-16: 2-6 miles (3 nearest schools)
  • 11-16: 2-15 miles (faith schools)

Current ABM Advantages:

  • Dynamic simulation: Can model household decision-making processes
  • Spatially explicit: MATSim captures actual transport networks
  • Counterfactual scenarios: Can test "what if" policy changes
  • Individual behaviour: Models each household's school choice

Hidden Complexity:

The Same Policy, Different Impacts:

Example: 11-year-old, low-income, 4 miles from school

‚úì White British: Likely uses local school (free transport)

‚úì Pakistani Muslim: May prefer faith school 8 miles away (not covered)

‚úì Black Caribbean: May avoid local "failing" school (complex choice)

Research Gap:

Current models simulate transport use but can't explain WHY different groups make different choices under the same policy

Key Innovation: Reveals not just WHO is eligible, but who actually BENEFITS and WHY some eligible groups don't access support
