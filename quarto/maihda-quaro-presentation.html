What is MAIHDA?

Multilevel Analysis of Individual Heterogeneity and Discriminatory Accuracy

Note

The “Gold Standard” for Quantitative Intersectional Analysis

Treats intersectional identities as random effects in multilevel models, revealing how multiple characteristics combine to create unique experiences beyond simple additive effects

Conventional Approach:

  • Examines factors separately
  • Assumes additive effects
  • Misses critical intersections
  • Generic policy recommendations

MAIHDA Approach:

  • Creates intersectional strata
  • Reveals multiplicative effects
  • Handles small cell sizes via shrinkage
  • Enables targeted interventions

Warning

Three Extended MAIHDA Applications:

  1. Spatial MAIHDA → School Segregation Study
  2. Longitudinal MAIHDA → Teacher Retention Study
  3. Policy Evaluation MAIHDA → Transport & Accessibility Study

Study 1: Educational Segregation

Current Approach: Limitations

Research Question

Which students experience educational segregation, and how does this vary across space and time?

Tip

Current Aggregate Approach:

  • Calculates: Segregation indices by ethnicity and area
  • Shows: Overall levels of ethnic concentration in schools
  • Misses: Which specific students attend segregated schools
  • Cannot reveal: Intersectional vulnerabilities

Current Method

  • Aggregate segregation indices
  • Treats ethnic groups as homogeneous
  • Cannot identify individual risk factors
  • Misses within-group heterogeneity

The Segregation Paradox

Aggregate data shows decreasing segregation, but which students still experience it?

Who Attends Segregated Schools? Current methods can’t answer:

  • Are Pakistani boys more affected than Pakistani girls?
  • Does social class intersect with ethnicity?
  • Where do vulnerable groups cluster?

Warning

Definition of Segregated School: Schools where >70% of students are from a single ethnic group (following Burgess & Wilson, 2005)

Important

Limitation

Aggregate approaches hide intersectional experiences - we need individual-level analysis to understand WHO experiences segregation, not just HOW MUCH segregation exists

Spatial MAIHDA Enhancement

Individual-Level Spatial MAIHDA Innovation

Method Enhancement:

  • Unit: Individual students (N = 3.2 million)
  • Outcome: Attends segregated school (binary)
  • 24 intersectional strata: Ethnicity × SES × Gender
  • Cross-classified with spatial units (LSOAs)
  • Monte Carlo: 10,000 simulations

\[\text{logit}(Y_{ijk}) = \gamma_0 + u_{0j}[\text{strata}] + v_{0k}[\text{LSOA}] + W \cdot \text{spatial\_effects}\]

Key Innovation: Reveals WHO is most likely to experience segregation, not just WHERE segregation exists

Probability of Attending Segregated School:

Intersectional Group Probability Odds Ratio
Pakistani × Low SES × Female 68.4% 3.24 [2.89-3.61]
Pakistani × Low SES × Male 71.2% 3.57 [3.18-3.99]
White × High SES × Female 21.1% Reference
Black × Low SES × Male 52.3% 2.48 [2.21-2.78]

Discriminatory Accuracy: ICC = 0.15
85% of variation is within intersectional groups - context matters more than identity alone

Important

Critical Finding

Monte Carlo reveals Pakistani low-SES boys have 71.2% [95% CI: 68.4%-73.8%] probability of attending segregated schools, with strong spatial clustering (Moran’s I = 0.82) in specific urban areas. This intersectional vulnerability was invisible in aggregate analyses.

Study 2: Teacher Retention

Current Approach: Tracking the 2011 Cohort

Traditional Survival Analysis

Current Method Limitations:

  • Cox model with additive effects
  • Assumes: ethnicity + gender + ITT = risk
  • Misses intersectional experiences
  • Cannot identify time-specific vulnerabilities

Tip

Hidden Variation: Overall retention rates mask huge differences:

  • Female: 16.54% - but ranges from 8% to 24% by intersectional group
  • No ITT: 9.75% - but some groups retain at 18%, others at 3%

But which teachers struggle most?

Important

Critical Limitation

These aggregate curves hide intersectional vulnerabilities. A Black male teacher without ITT in London has a fundamentally different trajectory than a White female teacher with ITT in rural areas - but current analysis treats them as parts of separate, additive categories.

Longitudinal MAIHDA Enhancement

Revealing Intersectional Career Trajectories

Longitudinal MAIHDA Innovation:

  • 200 intersectional strata: Ethnicity × Gender × ITT × Region
  • Time-varying hazards: Captures non-linear career patterns
  • Individual trajectories: Random slopes for each stratum
  • Critical periods: Statistically identified vulnerability windows

\[\text{logit}(\text{leaving}) \sim \text{poly}(\text{year}, 2) + (\text{poly}(\text{year}, 2) | \text{strata}) + (1|\text{school}) + (1|\text{teacher})\]

Key Finding: 78% of variation is within intersectional groups - individual context dominates

  • Risk Statistics
  • Key Insight
Intersectional Group Year 1 Risk Year 2 Risk Critical Period 11-Year Retention
Black × Male × Non-ITT × London 18% 31% Year 2 Crisis 3.2%
Pakistani × Female × Non-ITT × North 22% 28% Year 1 Crisis 4.8%
White × Female × ITT × North 8% 10% Gradual 23.4%

Important

Transformative Insight

Longitudinal MAIHDA reveals that “teacher retention” isn’t one problem but many distinct challenges. Black male non-ITT teachers in London face a Year 2 crisis (HR = 2.8, 95% CrI: [2.1-3.7]), while Pakistani female non-ITT teachers in the North struggle most in Year 1. This enables precisely targeted, time-specific interventions.

Study 3: London’s Transport Policy

Current Approach: Policy Complexity

Evaluating Complex Eligibility Criteria: Who Really Benefits?

London’s Free Transport Policy:

Standard Eligibility:

  • Under 8: >2 miles to school
  • 8+: >3 miles to school
  • No safe walking route
  • Special educational needs

Low-Income Eligibility:

  • 8-11: >2 miles (nearest school)
  • 11-16: 2-6 miles (3 nearest schools)
  • 11-16: 2-15 miles (faith schools)

Current ABM Limitations:

  • Models “average” low-income behavior
  • Can’t capture faith school preferences by ethnicity
  • Misses intersectional barriers

Hidden Complexity:

Tip

The Same Policy, Different Impacts:

Example: 11-year-old, low-income, 4 miles from school - ✓ White British: Likely uses local school (free transport) - ✓ Pakistani Muslim: May prefer faith school 8 miles away (not covered) - ✓ Black Caribbean: May avoid local “failing” school (complex choice)

Warning

Research Gap: Current models simulate transport use but can’t explain WHY different groups make different choices under the same policy

Key Questions ABM Can’t Answer:

  • Do faith school preferences create transport inequity?
  • Which intersectional groups fall through eligibility gaps?
  • How do cultural factors interact with distance rules?

Important

Critical Limitation

Agent-based models treat policy eligibility as binary (yes/no) but can’t reveal how intersectional identities shape actual benefit realization or uncover systematic exclusions

MAIHDA-Enhanced Policy Evaluation

Revealing Who Benefits and Who’s Left Behind

MAIHDA + ABM Integration:

  • MAIHDA: Identifies differential policy impacts by intersectional group
  • ABM: Simulates spatial dynamics and school competition
  • 60 strata: Ethnicity × Income × Car × Distance
  • Monte Carlo: 10,000 policy scenarios

\[Y_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1(\text{Eligible}) + \beta_2(\text{Distance}) + u_{1j}(\text{Policy} \times \text{Strata}) + (1|\text{LSOA}) + \varepsilon_{ij}\]

Key Innovation: Reveals not just WHO is eligible, but who actually BENEFITS and WHY some eligible groups don’t access support

Differential Policy Realization:

Intersectional Group Eligible Take-up Impact
Pakistani × Low Income × 4mi ✓ 42% +1.2 schools
White × Low Income × 4mi ✓ 78% +3.1 schools
Black × Low Income × 2.5mi ✗ - 0 schools
Pakistani × Low Income × Faith ✓* 68% +4.8 schools

*Eligible for faith school provision (2-15 miles)

Monte Carlo Policy Refinements

Scenario A: Reduce threshold to 2.5 miles for all
→ 18,000 more eligible
→ Reduces gap by 31%

Scenario B: Cultural liaison for take-up
→ Pakistani take-up: 42%→71%
→ Most cost-effective

Scenario C: Expand faith provision
→ Addresses preferences
→ Reduces segregation 24%

Important

Transformative Finding

MAIHDA reveals the policy works differently than intended - Pakistani families are eligible but face cultural/information barriers (42% take-up), while Black families in the 2.5-3 mile gap receive no support despite similar need. Monte Carlo simulations show targeted outreach would be 3x more cost-effective than expanding eligibility.
